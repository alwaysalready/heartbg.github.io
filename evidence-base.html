We're building an evidence base

 
 

We at Save our Safer Streets are challenging Tower Hamlet's new mayor and council to publish local data on emergency service response times, air quality and traffic congestion.

Not just waiting for the council, we're building an evidence base that shows the new street designs across the borough haven’t had the negative impacts the Mayor and others have claimed, and that their removal isn’t justified.

So far, our key findings show:

  • NO increase in ambulance response times attributable to the Low Traffic Neighbourhood (LTN) schemes in Bethnal Green, and some neighbourhoods within Bethnal Green’s LTN areas seeing improvements in response times.
  • No increase in fire service response times since the installation of the LTNs and other new road layouts. This is for all five wards in Tower Hamlets where LTNs have been installed.
  • NO increase in journey times on boundary roads attributable to the LTNs - journey times have increased on all roads at a similar rate. Eastbound journeys on Hackney Road have increased by 30 seconds at most, but this is likely to be for several reasons such as roadworks and LTNs in neighbouring Hackney.
  • Air quality is now dramatically better than it was in 2019, across all of Tower Hamlets, and the LTNs are not going to change this trend.

Tower Hamlets Council will make a decision on 28 September about whether to “re-open the roads”, based on the result of a public consultation. The consultation document included several statements about the new layouts, including claims of significant delays to emergency service vehicles, increased congestion, significant bus delays, higher levels of pollution and worsening air quality.

When the consultation launched, we were concerned that it didn’t mention any of the benefits of the street schemes, and didn’t provide evidence of any negative impacts. We began our own research using Freedom of Information requests (FoIs) and data already in the public domain.

Through our FoI requests we have gathered data from organisations including London Fire Brigade, London Ambulance Service, Transport for London and Hackney Council. Unfortunately Tower Hamlets Council has not yet responded, despite repeated requests and missed deadlines.

Today we are challenging the council to publish data that backs up its consultation statements. The evidence we’ve compiled suggests those statements are inaccurate, and they shouldn’t be used to justify the full re-opening of all roads in Tower Hamlets.

Our research has found:

Emergency service response times

The mayor’s consultation claimed without evidence that there have been “numerous reported incidents where emergency service responses have been hindered by physical road closures.”

The mayor’s consultation claimed without evidence that there have been “numerous reported incidents where emergency service responses have been hindered by physical road closures.” We’ve found this isn’t the case. Analysis of the Emergency Service response times for the first set of road closures to go in, in Bethnal Green, has shown there has been no overall increase in emergency response times for either London Fire Brigade or the London Ambulance Service when compared to other areas of Tower Hamlets.

For ambulance response times, the data shows no measurable worsening that can be attributed to the LTN. For Category 1 response times (the highest category of emergencies) there are no significant differences before and after the LTNs were put in place. And, in fact, although Category 2 (less critical) ambulance response times in Tower Hamlets and reportedly across the UK have become slower since December 2020, the slow downs have been LESS in the areas surrounding Bethnal Green’s LTNs. This suggests the LTNs might actually be helping ambulances.

Our data from the fire services is much more comprehensive. We’ve analysed data for response times across all of Tower Hamlets and focused in particular for the five wards with new road layouts: Bow West, Spitalfields and Banglatown, St Katherine's and Wapping, St Peter's and Weavers. [option 1:] In all but one ward, average response times have fallen since the new road layouts. In St Peter’s there has been just a one second increase in average response times. This, however, is not statistically significant. In all cases, having run statistical tests, we can say average response times for the Fire Services have stayed stable. [option 2:] Having run statistical tests, we’ve discovered in all wars average response times for the Fire Services have stayed stable. In other words, the LTNs have had no difference to fire engine response times.

The data on fire service response times shows that for Tower Hamlets overall, and for the E2 postcode, Weavers and St Peter’s wards, there is very little change in response times before and after the LTN schemes were put in place (Jan 2019 – May 2021). The biggest delay recorded was in St Peter’s ward of just 15 seconds, and several areas actually showed improved response times.

The claim that there have been numerous reported incidents is not backed up by the data either.

Out of the 14,756 Cat 1 and 2 (life-threatening / emergency) ambulance calls that have been made to the postcodes covered by the Bethnal Green LTN since 2019, only nine have had delays attributed in any way to the Liveable Streets scheme – 0.06% of all calls.

While any delay is less than ideal, this information shows it is a small problem with less than 6 in 10,000 calls experiencing any kind of recorded delay that can be attributed to the LTNs. There is also no evidence that these delays are an ongoing problem to the London Ambulance Service as they have not identified the cause of any delays.

We know that in the early days of the LTNs, there were some issues around parts of the scheme as well as problems with ambulance crews not knowing the new layout. Tower Hamlets council amended the scheme soon after it was put in, in response to feedback from the emergency services. Further evidence would be needed to support claims that delays have persisted.

Traffic and congestion

Another claim in the consultation document is that “congestion on Hackney Road and Bethnal Green Road (has) increased.” Congestion is best and most easily measured using TFL bus journey times. Our analysis of bus journey times and other data provided by TFL shows that on almost all boundary roads near the LTNS, changes in journey times are in line with the general trend on similar roads (without LTNs) in Tower Hamlets.

Congestion is best and most easily measured using TFL bus journey times. Our analysis of bus journey times and other data provided by TFL shows that on almost all boundary roads near the LTNS, changes in journey times are in line with the general trend on similar roads (without LTNs) in Tower Hamlets.

There has been slight slowing in the Eastbound stretch of Hackney Road, but the data shows that at most bus journey times have increased by an average of 30 seconds.

This road receives a lot of traffic from bordering Hackney, which has also brought in changes to traffic management in recent years. It is not possible or credible to attribute the slight slowing of traffic to the Tower Hamlets LTN.

Hackney Road and Bethnal Green Road have always been two of the slower roads in the borough for bus journeys. Since the LTNs were installed, Hackney Road eastbound has slowed by less than 1mph and Bethnal Green Road has seen no change – while Whitechapel Road, far from the LTN zone, has become slower than either of them.

Another trend is that journey times, having slowed in 2021 / 22, are now improving again. This may indicate that things are settling down after the introduction of the LTNs and motorists are adapting. At the very least, it shows that the picture is more nuanced than this particular LTN = busier boundary roads.

Traffic patterns in Tower Hamlets have shifted around a great deal in the last couple of years for many reasons. Besides the installations of LTNs in Tower Hamlets we have seen several lockdowns, new ULEZ rules, and more cars on the roads across the borough than in 2019. None of the evidence we have seen supports the claim that congestion has increased enough to justify reversing the LTNs – especially when you look at air quality.

A letter released to us through an FOI request to TFL also points out that reversing the changes on the Old Bethnal Green Road scheme, will potentially also have an impact on bus journey times and congestion on Hackney Road. In an official letter to Lutfur Rahman, dated 28 July this year, Will Norman from TFL stated:

“...Removing the Old Bethnal Road scheme would prevent TfL introducing a bus priority scheme on Hackney Road as cyclists who currently benefit from the scheme would be forced back to use that road.”

Air quality

The bottom line on air quality in Tower Hamlets is that it is dramatically better now that it was a few years ago – according to the council’s own data.

Everywhere in the borough now has lower pollution levels than in 2019 and is within air quality objectives. Only Roman Road / Globe Road and Whitechapel High St have seen any increase in pollution since 2020, and both are still far below 2019 levels.

It’s too early for accurate data for 2022, but up until the end of 2021 there was no negative impact on air quality on boundary roads from the Old Bethnal Green Road LTN.

Conclusion

In the absence of evidence that the changes to road layouts have had negative impacts on emergency service response times, congestion and air quality, the question of whether to reverse the new road layouts becomes a matter of personal preference and personal convenience for motorists. This needs to be weighed carefully against improved safety, the needs of the whole community and the wider climate crisis.

It always takes time to get used to change. There may be improvements that can be made to the street schemes which address residents’ concerns, but ripping out the entire scheme without evidence that it’s not working would be hugel wasteful and unnecessarily extreme.
